## Thesis worth preserving

Alphabet can be analyzed as a corporate capital-allocation system: a highly profitable advertising-led operating core finances a portfolio of long-duration businesses and research programs. Berkshire is useful as a bounded analogy for comparing funding sources, decentralization, governance, reinvestment discipline, and the limits of conglomerate structure. It is not an identity claim. The rebuilt article should ask where the analogy explains reality, where it fails, and what evidence would change the conclusion.

## Primary-source plan

1. **Alphabet 10-K filings:** corporate structure; Google Services/Cloud/Other Bets segment economics; advertising revenue; operating cash flow, capex, R&D, repurchases, cash and marketable securities; risk factors; legal contingencies.
2. **Alphabet 10-Q filings:** accepted-current-through update for the latest quarter; changes in revenue mix, liquidity, segment losses, capex, repurchases, and material legal proceedings.
3. **Alphabet earnings releases and investor materials:** management's period-specific explanations and reconciliations. Use only materials hosted by Alphabet Investor Relations or filed as exhibits.
4. **Alphabet proxy/governance materials:** voting control, board structure, shareholder proposals, executive incentives, and capital-allocation governance. Do not infer activist influence from generic governance commentary.
5. **Berkshire shareholder letters and annual reports:** float balance/cost/duration, underwriting risk, operating-company cash generation, acquisition criteria, decentralization, and capital-allocation statements.
6. **Authoritative regulatory/legal records:** filed complaints, judgments, remedies, and agency releases from DOJ, FTC where applicable, European Commission, CMA, and court dockets/opinions. Commentary may explain but cannot serve as the factual anchor.

Tangential marketplace essays, generic diversification notes, and broad increasing-returns articles may appear only in a clearly labeled interpretation/sidebar. They cannot support Alphabet financial, governance, operating, or legal claims.

## Proposed article outline

1. **The claim, bounded:** what “Berkshire 2.0” means and does not mean.
2. **The cash engine:** dated Alphabet segment mix, operating cash generation, capex, R&D, and distributions.
3. **Where the cash goes:** disclosed Other Bets economics, acquisitions, repurchases, and investment—without inventing internal hurdle rates.
4. **Float versus operating cash:** a direct comparison of funding mechanics, liabilities, taxes, cyclicality, and governance.
5. **Governance and control:** Alphabet voting structure and board/accountability facts versus Berkshire's structure.
6. **Concentration and regulation:** advertising dependence plus named, current legal exposures.
7. **Where the analogy breaks:** project heterogeneity, disclosure limits, and the absence of Berkshire-style acquisition evidence.
8. **What would change the thesis:** measurable disconfirming evidence and accepted-current-through state.
